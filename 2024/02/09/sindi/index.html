What is paralysis?

Sindi Ismaili
2024·02·09

Paralysis is the temporary or permanent inability to move a part of the body. In almost all cases the paralysis is due to nerve damage rather than an injury to the affected region. For example, an injury to the middle or lower region of the spinal cord is likely to disrupt function below the injured part, including the ability to move the legs or feel sensation even though the actual structures are as healthy as ever.

What causes paralysis? • Spinal cord injuries: When the nerves in the spinal cord are compressed or damaged, the spinal cord cannot send or receive signals to the area that these nerves communicate with; • Brain injuries: Brain injuries, including traumatic blows to the head and brain lesions, interfere with the brain's ability to send and receive signals to other parts of the body; • Non-traumatic injuries: These can include brain attacks, tumors and oxygen deprivation can damage the brain or spinal cord paralyzing part of the body.

Other common causes of paralysis include: • Multiple sclerosis (17%); • Cerebral palsy (7%); • Post-polio syndrome (5%); • Traumatic brain injury (4%); • Neurofibromatosis (4%); • Birth defects (2%)
